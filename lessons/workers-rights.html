Workers Rights Most Employers Don't Tell You About

Federal law gives workers significant protections. Most employers don't volunteer this information. Here are the ones that matter most — and what to do when they're violated.

You can discuss your salary with coworkers

The National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) protects "concerted activity" — which includes talking about pay. Policies that prohibit salary discussion are illegal for most private sector workers. This right exists even if you're not in a union.

Tip pooling has strict rules

Employers and managers cannot take a share of tip pools. Tips belong to workers. Violations: file with the Department of Labor Wage and Hour Division — dol.gov/agencies/whd. Recovery includes back tips + equal amount in damages.

Misclassification as a contractor is often illegal

If you're treated like an employee (set hours, specific tools required, single employer), you may legally be an employee regardless of what your contract says. Misclassified workers can recover unpaid overtime, benefits, and employer-side taxes. File with the IRS (Form SS-8) or state labor board.

Unpaid wage recovery has no upfront cost

Wage theft attorneys work on contingency — you pay nothing unless you win. Under the FLSA, employers pay attorney fees if you win. This means free legal representation for unpaid overtime, minimum wage violations, and off-the-clock work claims.

FMLA gives you 12 weeks of job-protected leave

If you've worked for an employer with 50+ employees for 12 months and 1,250 hours, you're entitled to 12 weeks of unpaid, job-protected leave per year for serious health conditions, family care, or birth/adoption. They cannot fire you for taking it.

Retaliation for complaints is illegal

OSHA, FLSA, NLRA, and most other labor laws have anti-retaliation provisions. If you file a complaint and are fired, demoted, or otherwise punished, that's a separate violation often worth more than the original claim. Document everything before you file.

Non-competes are often unenforceable

The FTC ruled in 2024 to ban most non-competes. Even before that ruling, non-competes for low-wage workers were unenforceable in many states (CA, ND, OK, MN ban them entirely). Talk to an employment attorney before assuming you're bound. Initial consultations are often free.

Where to file

Wage theft / overtime / misclassification: Department of Labor — dol.gov/agencies/whd

Workplace safety: OSHA — osha.gov/workers

Discrimination: EEOC — eeoc.gov

Union organizing / NLRA violations: NLRB — nlrb.gov

All free to file. All have retaliation protections.

✅ The documentation rule

Before you do anything: document. Screenshot pay stubs. Save emails. Write down dates, times, what was said, who was present. Evidence wins cases. Memories are insufficient.
