Before you file

Send a demand letter first. One page. State what they owe, why, and that you'll file in small claims if not paid within 14 days. Send certified mail. This is sometimes required by the court. It also often gets you paid without ever filing.

Keep a copy. The date-stamped certified receipt becomes evidence.

How to file

  1. Go to your county courthouse clerk's office (or their website ‚Äî many now accept online filings)
  2. Ask for small claims forms. They'll give them to you. Fill out who you're suing, for how much, and why in plain language
  3. Pay the filing fee ($30‚Äì$100 depending on state ‚Äî added to your judgment if you win)
  4. The court serves the defendant ‚Äî they mail the summons for you
  5. You get a hearing date. Usually 30‚Äì70 days out

Preparing your case

Judges see many cases. Be organized. Bring:

  • Your demand letter + certified mail receipt
  • Any contracts, receipts, invoices
  • Photos (timestamped)
  • Text/email evidence printed out
  • A one-page timeline of events
  • Any witnesses (tell them the date ahead of time)
‚ö†Ô∏è The one rule

Don't exaggerate. Judges are very good at spotting embellishment. State only what you can prove. A credible, documented $800 claim wins. An inflated $2,000 claim often loses.

The hearing

You each get 5‚Äì10 minutes to speak. The judge may ask questions. Don't interrupt the other side. Address the judge as "Your Honor." Speak to facts, not feelings.

Most hearings take under 30 minutes. Many judges rule the same day.

Collecting your judgment

Winning the judgment isn't the same as getting paid. If they don't pay voluntarily:

  • Bank account levy (court orders their bank to pay you)
  • Wage garnishment (their employer deducts from paycheck)
  • Property lien (attaches to their real estate)

Ask the clerk how to file a writ of execution in your state. It's another form. Usually $25‚Äì$50 more.

Most defendants, especially businesses, will settle before the hearing once they receive the summons. The filing fee alone often gets you paid. File first, negotiate after.
